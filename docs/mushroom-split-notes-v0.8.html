Mushroom breakup notes

This revision replaces several too-broad mushroom buckets with field-useful species groups or complexes. The goal is to stop flattening things like boletes, russulas, chanterelles, and oysters into mushy one-size-fits-all entries.

Morels
Removed the generic “morel mushrooms” bucket and added half-free morels as their own eastern true-morel entry. Black morels and yellow morels stay separate.
Chanterelles
Replaced the generic chanterelle bucket with hardwood yellow chanterelles, conifer yellow chanterelles, red chanterelles, and smooth chanterelles. Yellowfoot and black trumpet stay as their own entries.
Russulas
Replaced the generic edible-russula bucket with shrimp russulas, charcoal burners / mild flexible-gill russulas, and short-stemmed russula.
Boletes
Replaced the generic bolete bucket with scaber-stalk boletes, bitter bolete, and a blue-staining / red-pored caution group. King bolete, slippery jack, and larch suillus remain separate.
Oysters
Replaced the generic oyster bucket with pearl oyster, phoenix oyster, and aspen oyster.

Practical rule: when current taxonomy is unsettled or regional data are messy, the app now prefers a named complex or field group over pretending one exact species name is settled.

Half-free morels

Morchella punctipes (eastern half-free morel)

A true morel with the cap attached only about halfway down the stem, leaving the lower half hanging free. Excellent edible when thoroughly cooked.

Why this split: listed separately because eastern half-free morels are a recognizable true-morel type in hardwood forests east of the Great Plains

Research notes
  • The eastern half-free morel is Morchella punctipes; the western counterpart is Morchella populiphila.
  • Treat this as its own true-morel variant rather than hiding it under generic morels.
  • Strong confusion warning should still point users away from false morels like Gyromitra.

Yellow chanterelles (hardwood-associated complex)

Cantharellus spp. (hardwood-associated yellow chanterelles)

Golden to yellow chanterelles associated with hardwoods, especially oak and beech. Fruity, apricot-like aroma and decurrent false gills are key field marks.
